EASEE'S Mission

__________________________________________________________________________________________

The Education and Actions for Sustainable Energy Efficiency (EASEE) empowers students (and, through them, their families and communities) to effect positive change on the environment by educating them and giving them strategies to share their knowledge and implement their ideas. 

 

 

EASEE'S Approach

__________________________________________________________________________________________


EASEE programs are designed to help families connect every energy decision they make to reducing carbon emissions and lowering utility costs.


Our program emphasizes environmental education to promote energy efficiency in local communities through the actions of students. Our impact is local as well as global because we help the students understand the role their actions play in saving the planet. Once students become EASEE Energy Experts, they have the means to effect change and improve our climate. 

Our efforts promote active engagement in conscientious, responsible use of energy to further foster and fuel a lifelong awareness and commitment to energy efficiency and environmental action. EASEE also seeks to bring direct economic benefit to participating student-families. Our initial program will save student-families $150 in avoided electricity expense.

EASEE History

__________________________________________________________________________________________


In the late 1990s the founder of EASEE began to teach elementary school children some basic energy savings tips and to give away Compact Fluorescent Light Bulbs for them to use in their own homes. The approach was successful in reaching students and to date we have distributed over a thousand CFLs. In 2009, we began to formalize our program. Now fully incorporated as an Illinois not-for-profit corporation with 501 (c)(3) designation, EASEE is working with school children in the Chicago area. By the end of the 2010-2011 academic year EASEE plans to have brought the program to over 1,500 students elementary and middle school students in Chicago.
